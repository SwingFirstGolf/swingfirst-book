SwingFirst — Internal Strategy

Building the World's Golf Tee Time Marketplace

A clear-eyed look at real-time data acquisition, the full build roadmap, booking attribution, and the strategic questions that will determine whether SwingFirst wins.

Prepared: April 2026 Confidential — SwingFirst Golf Group
Question 1

How do we get real-time tee time availability for the world's public golf courses — with zero manual processes?

This is the hardest technical problem SwingFirst faces, and the most important one to get right. The answer has three parts: who owns the booking infrastructure, how you access it, and in what order you attack it.

The booking platform landscape

Golf courses don't each build their own booking systems — the vast majority use one of a handful of platforms. This is actually good news. If you can access ten platforms, you can cover most of the world's public courses that sell tee times online.

GolfNow / NBC Sports
~10,000+
Dominant in the US. Has an affiliate API — but using it makes SwingFirst a GolfNow affiliate, which fundamentally undermines the "no fees" positioning. Scraping is more strategically consistent, though legally riskier.
TeeOff / EZLinks
~4,000+
Second largest US platform, acquired by Acushnet. Has structured booking pages amenable to scraping. No meaningful public API.
ForeUp
~1,500+
Popular mid-market US platform. Booking widget is embedded on course sites. Structured, consistent HTML makes it very scrapeable.
Club Caddie
~800+
Growing US platform. Similar to ForeUp in structure.
ClubV1 / BRS Golf
~2,000+
Dominant in the UK. Powers most British courses. BRS Golf (acquired by EZLinks) has a consistent booking interface.
Intelligent Golf
~500+
UK-focused. Consistent widget. Highly scrapeable.
Chrono Golf
~3,000+
Dominant in France and parts of Europe. Essential for European coverage.
Custom / proprietary
~20%
Larger clubs, resort courses, and municipal systems. Require course-specific scrapers or SwingFirst Tee Sheet adoption.

The acquisition strategy: scraping at scale

The honest answer is web scraping — automated browsers that visit booking pages, extract available tee times and prices, and feed that data into SwingFirst's cache. This is exactly what Kayak did to airlines, Google Flights did to everyone, and what virtually every travel aggregator is built on. It is legally gray (terms of service violation, not a crime), and it is industrially standard practice.

The way you do it without manual processes is by targeting platforms, not individual courses. You build one scraper for GolfNow's booking widget — which looks the same for every GolfNow course — and that one scraper covers 10,000+ courses instantly. Same for EZLinks, ForeUp, BRS. You're not writing custom scrapers per course; you're writing platform-level scrapers that scale horizontally.

The technical stack for this: Playwright (headless Chromium) to handle JavaScript-rendered booking pages, a job queue (Redis/BullMQ) to schedule scraping runs per course, a cache layer (Redis with 15-minute TTL) to serve data without re-scraping on every search, and a monitoring layer that alerts when a scraper breaks (platforms change their HTML regularly).

URL discovery and verification — fully automated

To know which URL to scrape for each course, you need to first know what booking platform they use. Here's how to do that automatically:

Step 1 — Course database seed. Start with a compiled database of public golf courses. The USGA maintains one. Google Places API returns golf courses by location and includes their website URLs. Combining these gives you a near-complete global course list with websites.

Step 2 — Platform fingerprinting. Visit each course's website with a headless browser and look for known booking widget signatures. GolfNow widgets contain specific JavaScript calls and iframes with recognizable domain patterns. EZLinks, ForeUp, BRS all have distinct fingerprints. One pass through each course's website classifies ~80% of them automatically.

Step 3 — Booking URL discovery. Once you know the platform, you know the URL pattern. GolfNow's booking URLs follow a consistent structure (golfnow.com/courses/{id}/reservations). ForeUp's embed URL contains the course's ForeUp ID. You extract these from the fingerprinting step.

Step 4 — Validation. After extracting the URL, make one test scrape. If you get back tee time data in the expected format, the URL is verified. If not, flag for retry. No human involved.

Step 5 — Ongoing monitoring. Re-validate each URL weekly. If a course switches platforms, the fingerprinting step will catch it on the next weekly pass.

The strategic priority order
Start with your 6 SwingFirst courses (direct data access, zero scraping needed). Then add GolfNow coverage (10,000+ courses in one scraper). Then EZLinks/TeeOff, ForeUp, BRS Golf (UK). Those four platforms alone cover roughly 70% of public golf courses in the US and UK. Custom and proprietary systems come later — drive those courses toward the SwingFirst Tee Sheet instead.
Legal risk to be aware of
Scraping violates the terms of service of most platforms. This creates civil (not criminal) risk — typically a cease-and-desist, not a lawsuit. The standard industry response is to move faster than the legal system and negotiate partnerships once you have leverage. GolfNow will eventually notice and send a C&D. The correct response is to use it as a forcing function to negotiate a data partnership on your terms — at which point you have traffic they want. Get a lawyer to review before going to production scale.

How this gets built

No engineers. Dave and Claude write every line of code together, with Dave as product owner and decision-maker and Claude handling all technical implementation. This is not a constraint — it's the model. There is no communication gap between product and engineering, no sprint queue, no handoff delay.

The division of labor is simple: Claude writes the code during each session; Dave handles the button-clicking that only a human with real accounts can do (Supabase project setup, Stripe dashboard, Vercel deploy). Each of those steps takes an afternoon with a precise checklist, not a week of dev time. The mockup-first methodology — refine in HTML until the product is exactly right, then wire to real backend — means every build decision is already validated before a line of production code is written.

Timelines above are measured in days and weeks of daily sessions, not engineering sprints.

What "tested" means before going live

Before sending traffic to real golf courses, you need to verify: that booking URLs actually resolve to the right pages (we built the link checker for this), that scraped tee times are accurate (spot-check by actually visiting the booking pages yourself), that the claim flow works end-to-end including Stripe payment, that the dashboard shows accurate data, and that search results are geographically correct within your radius logic. Your 6 courses are the test bed — if it works perfectly for them, extend to others.

The fastest path to revenue
Don't wait for the platform to be "complete." Get Phase 1 live and charge your first external premium listing. One paying customer at $149/month validates the whole model. The scraping, the tee sheet, the international expansion — all of that can happen while you already have a live, revenue-generating product.
Question 3

Can we track whether golfers who clicked through actually booked a tee time?

This is one of the hardest problems in digital advertising, and it's not unique to SwingFirst — it's the same challenge every marketplace faces when it sends traffic to third-party websites. The honest answer is: you cannot perfectly track confirmed bookings for courses on external booking systems. But you can get meaningfully close, and for courses on the SwingFirst Tee Sheet you can track every single booking with 100% certainty.

Here's every tracking method available to you, ordered by accuracy:

SwingFirst Tee Sheet (Track B courses)
100% accurate
Every booking flows through SwingFirst's system. You know exactly who booked, when, for how much, and from what search. This is the only method that gives you confirmed booking data — and it's the most powerful argument for the Tee Sheet product. Track A gives you a listing; Track B gives you a revenue-sharing partnership. Frame it that way.
Referral pixel / server-side postback
High (requires course cooperation)
Ask courses to place a small JavaScript snippet on their booking confirmation page. When a golfer books after clicking from SwingFirst, the snippet fires back to SwingFirst with the booking value. This is how affiliate marketing has worked for 25 years. The challenge: many small golf courses don't have the technical capability to install a pixel, and some won't bother. Make it a one-line script with copy-paste instructions. Offer a discount or bonus analytics for courses that implement it.
UTM / referral click tracking
Clicks only, not bookings
Append ?ref=swingfirst&utm_source=swingfirst to every booking link. You can see in SwingFirst's analytics how many people clicked "Book →", and courses can see the referral traffic in their own Google Analytics. This proves you're driving traffic, but not whether it converted to a booking. For Track A courses, this is your primary proof-of-value metric — clicks sent, not bookings made. Be honest about this distinction with course operators; they'll respect the transparency.
Redirect link tracking
Clicks only (more reliable than UTM)
Instead of linking directly to swingfirstgolf.com/teetimes, link through swingfirst.co/go/palmbrook. Your server records the click, then redirects. This is more reliable than UTM because it can't be blocked by cookie settings or ad blockers, and it works even if the course's booking system strips URL parameters.
Post-click user survey
Low (but provides signal)
After a user clicks "Book →", show a subtle prompt when they return to SwingFirst: "Did you find a tee time? Let us know so we can improve." A small percentage of users will respond. Directionally useful but not statistically reliable. Good for early-stage qualitative feedback.
The strategic implication
The tracking limitation for Track A courses is actually a feature, not a bug. Use it as your most compelling sales argument for the Tee Sheet. Tell every Track A course: "We can show you how many golfers we send your way. To see how many actually booked — and to share in the revenue from every booking we drive — move to SwingFirst Tee Sheet." The upgrade path sells itself.
Question 4

Other things you should be thinking about

⚔️
GolfNow will respond — and that's fine
GolfNow is owned by NBC Sports and charges courses 20–50% of the round price for tee times sold through their platform. Courses hate this but feel trapped. SwingFirst's "zero booking fees, book direct" positioning is the single most powerful differentiator in the market. When GolfNow notices you and sends a cease-and-desist about scraping, treat it as a validation milestone. By that point you should have enough traffic to negotiate a data deal on your terms, or pivot to relying entirely on your own tee sheet data.
🐓
The chicken-and-egg problem is real — here's how to solve it
Golfers won't use SwingFirst without comprehensive tee time data. Courses won't pay for premium listings without golfer traffic. The way you break this is the same way every marketplace has broken it: manufacture supply first. Your 6 owned courses are the supply seed. Add scraped data to create the illusion of density before you have organic listings. Then use that data to sell the first 10 external premium listings. Then use those listings to attract more golfers. The flywheel starts slowly and then accelerates.
📱
Mobile is where the bookings actually happen
Most golfers decide to play within 24–48 hours of the round, often on their phone. The current mockup has responsive CSS but isn't truly optimized for mobile. The search experience, pill layout, and booking handoff all need to feel native on a 375px screen. This should be a Phase 1 requirement, not a Phase 3 polish item. A golfer standing in a parking lot on a Saturday morning is your highest-intent user — don't lose them to a broken mobile layout.
🔍
SEO is your biggest free acquisition channel
Searches like "golf courses in Phoenix," "tee times Scottsdale this weekend," "cheap golf near London" have enormous search volume and strong commercial intent. If SwingFirst builds location-specific landing pages (one per major golf market, updated with real tee time data), Google will index them and send organic traffic at zero marginal cost. This requires the web app to generate static or server-side-rendered pages per city — a Next.js architectural decision that should be made in Phase 1, not retrofitted later.
💰
Reconsider the pricing — $1.50/booking scales better than you think
A Phoenix-area course doing 150 rounds/day × 365 days × $1.50 = $82,125/year from one course. But not all those rounds come from SwingFirst — initially maybe 5–10% do. At 10%, that's $8,212/year per course, still far better than $149/month ($1,788/year). As SwingFirst grows and drives more traffic, the per-booking revenue grows proportionally. This is why the Tee Sheet model is so powerful: your revenue scales with your success at driving bookings, which aligns perfectly with the course's interests. Consider whether $1.50 is the right number or whether 2% (which scales with green fee prices) is smarter.
🏌️
Data freshness will make or break trust
Showing a tee time that's already been booked is worse than showing no tee time at all. A golfer who clicks through to find the slot is gone and who tells their friends SwingFirst shows bad data. The scraping cache TTL should be aggressive — 10–15 minutes for popular courses during peak times, longer for off-peak. For Tee Sheet courses, you can push real-time availability since you own the data. For scraped courses, be honest in the UI: show "Last updated 12 minutes ago" rather than pretending the data is live. Golfers appreciate honesty.
🌍
International is a strategic advantage, not a distraction
GolfNow is predominantly US-focused. There is no credible global tee time aggregator. SwingFirst's London listings aren't a nice-to-have — they're a genuine competitive moat that GolfNow doesn't have. The BRS Golf scraper (which covers most UK courses) gets you to comprehensive UK coverage fast. After UK, the next markets by golf participation rate and online booking penetration are Australia, Canada, and the major European markets (Germany, France, Sweden). Don't be too conservative about how big this can get.
⚖️
Get a lawyer before you go to production
Two specific things need legal review before you go live at scale: (1) the web scraping terms of service issue — you want to understand your actual risk exposure before scraping 10,000 courses; and (2) the legal attestation language in the claim flow — the language we've written is directionally right but needs a real lawyer to make it enforceable. Both of these are one-time costs, not ongoing. Budget a few thousand dollars for a startup IP attorney early. It's far cheaper than a C&D after you're live.
🏆
The endgame — what winning looks like
The most valuable version of SwingFirst is not an advertising platform for golf courses — it's the operating system for public golf. If 20% of the world's public courses run on SwingFirst's tee sheet, SwingFirst has a defensible, scalable, high-margin business that no competitor can replicate without rebuilding the entire customer relationship from scratch. Every course that adopts the tee sheet is a customer that becomes structurally expensive to replace. That's the moat. Everything before that — the search, the listings, the marketplace — is how you earn the right to pitch the tee sheet.
